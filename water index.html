üíß Water Management in Maharashtra: Report on Current Practices and Future Planning

PROJECT FOCUS

A comprehensive evaluation of Maharashtra's water governance and policy frameworks (MWRRA, MMISF, and Irrigation Acts) alongside an analysis of conservation effectiveness and utilization across diverse agro-climatic regions, supported by district-level case studies on water stress in Beed, Buldhana, Latur, Raigad, and Solapur.

Objectives:

  • Assess Water Utilization: Analyze the patterns of water allocation and distribution across various sectors and agro-climatic regions in Maharashtra
  • Evaluate Governance Frameworks: Examine the effectiveness of the MWRRA Act (2005), MMISF Act (2005), and the Maharashtra Irrigation Act (1976) in managing state water resources
  • Review Institutional Structures: Identify strengths and gaps in the institutional frameworks governing water distribution and management
  • Analyze Conservation Impact: Evaluate the implementation and success rates of various water conservation strategies and projects across the state

Key Findings:

  • Legislative Review: While the MWRRA Act (2005) provides a solid regulatory foundation, implementation gaps persist in equitable water distribution
  • Regional Disparities: Significant imbalance in water availability across agro-climatic zones, with rain-shadow regions facing chronic scarcity compared to coastal belts
  • District-Level Stress Patterns:
    • Beed & Latur: Severe groundwater depletion with heavy reliance on external water tankers during summer
    • Solapur & Buldhana: Critical need for crop-pattern shifts to align with irrigation potential
    • Raigad: "Functional scarcity" despite high rainfall due to rapid runoff and inadequate localized storage
  • Institutional Gaps: Water Users' Associations (WUAs) require further empowerment and financial autonomy for improved "tail-end" water delivery

Key Recommendations:

  • Policy Harmonization: Modernize the Maharashtra Irrigation Act (1976) to better integrate contemporary climate-resilience and groundwater regulation
  • Decentralized Storage: Focus on small-scale conservation structures in districts like Raigad to capture monsoon runoff
  • Demand-Side Management: Implement mandatory micro-irrigation for water-intensive crops in water-stressed districts
  • Strengthen WUAs: Enhance capacity building for participatory irrigation management and local network maintenance

üåä Sal River Water Quality Analysis: Longitudinal Assessment & Management Strategy

PROJECT FOCUS

A longitudinal assessment of the Sal River's 35‚Äì40 km stretch across five strategic monitoring locations to analyze its unique hydrological transition into a 14 km tidal zone and evaluate the impact of urbanization and climate change on its eco-sensitive mangrove and khazan ecosystems.

Objectives:

  • Long-term Water Quality Assessment: Compare data from 2016-17 to 2023-24 to identify trends in chemical and biological pollutants
  • Action Plan Evaluation: Measure effectiveness of ‚Çπ42.37 crore sewage infrastructure and ‚Çπ25.12 crore environmental flow interventions
  • Root Cause Identification: Investigate drivers of salinization including desilting operations, monsoon variability, and illegal discharges

Key Findings:

  • Biological Recovery vs. Chemical Decline: Khareband saw 94.8% reduction in fecal coliform due to sewage infrastructure, yet experienced 6,695% rise in chloride levels
  • Catastrophic Salinization: Pazorconi (mid-river tributary) showed unprecedented 9,069% increase in conductivity, shifting from freshwater to brackish habitat
  • Respiratory Crisis: Dissolved Oxygen (DO) at Orlim Bridge dropped 28.7% to 4.58 mg/L, nearing critical 4.0 mg/L threshold for aquatic life
  • Inconsistent Recovery: Mobor showed 91.8% decrease in chlorides, but Orlim Bridge faced 153.8% increase in fecal coliform indicating new contamination sources

Key Recommendations:

  • Transition from Infrastructure to Source-Tracking: Shift from general engineering to active real-time monitoring and source-tracking at biological hotspots like Orlim Bridge
  • Enhance Environmental Flows: Current treated effluent levels from STPs are insufficient against tidal pressure; higher freshwater discharge needed
  • Climate-Resilient Management: Account for sea-level rise and aggressive tidal penetration likely exacerbated by past desilting operations
  • Integrated Remediation: Beyond sewage treatment, enforce stricter controls against illegal stormwater discharges and localized point-source pollution
